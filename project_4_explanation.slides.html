Loan Annual Percentage Rate¶

Loan APRs range from 0.0065% up to 0.51%, with a median value of 0.21%.

Loan Amount¶

Amounts borrowed range from \$1,000 to \$35,000, with a median amount of \$6,500.

Loan Term (length)¶

  • 77% are 36 months
  • 22% are 60 months
  • 1% are 12 months

Credit Score*¶

The credit scores range from 360 up to 880, with a median credit score of 680.

*Note: The data reports two credit score values, a 'lower' and an 'upper' value. Every 'upper' value is +19 points above the corresponding 'lower' value. The reported values here are the 'lower' values.

Risk Assessment Score¶

Risk assessment scores go from 1 (highest risk) up to 11 (lowest risk). The median value is 6.

Importance of Loan Rate¶

Loan Rate is very important, as a higher rate means you are required to pay back more money, above what you borrowed, for the ability to borrow that money. The lending institution gives a Risk Assessment Score to every borrower. This number is used to gage the loan risk and to set up loan conditions so that the lender minimizes its risk.

Importance of Loan Rate (cont.)¶

From the previous graph, the median Loan Rate trend is clearly correlated with Risk Assessment Score. With a Risk Score of 1, the median loan rate is 0.35%. Loans with a Risk Score of 10 or 11 have a median rate of 0.10%, 3.5 times lower. Comparing a loan with a Risk Score of 10 or 11 to an average risk loan (6), the higher risk score gives a loan rate of half.

Risk Score and Credit Score¶

The linear regression shows the trend between Risk Score and Credit Score, as positive. There are many cases of individuals having a high credit score and recieving a low risk score (considered higher risk). And there are examples of the opposite occuring, but the trend is clear.

Credit Score and Homeownership¶

Lending institutions might see homeownership as a positive, indicating that they are more likely to be able to payback their loan. From the next graph, the median credit score of a homeowner is 700, compared to 680 of non-homeowners.

Loan Rate and Credit Score¶

One would expect that, on average, a person with a higher credit score would be able to get a lower loan rate.

Loan Rate and Credit Score (cont.)¶

Loan Rate and Credit Score have an inverse relationship. There are cases that stray from the expected, but the trend is clear. There are other factors that determine a loan rate, other than credit score, but it is an important factor.

Monthly Payment, Loan Amount, Term Length¶

Three distinct cases emerge from the graph, one for each term length. It makes sense that as the loan amount increases that the corresponding monthly payments also increase. Also, as the term length increases (lighter in color), for a constant loan amount, that the monthly payments also decrease.

Monthly Payment, Loan Amount, Loan APR¶

For a given loan amount, the lower the loan rate (darker in color), the lower the monthly payments are.
